The author describes a number of considerations in justifying the sample size of a study. These considerations pertain to the sample size, the achieved power, the assumed effect size in power analysis, and even the alpha threshold. Some of these considerations are old and worth repeating, and others have previously been relatively neglected. In a way, the manuscriptâ€™s greatest strength is also its greatest weakness: a great many issues and considerations are described in detail, but there are so many considerations that the prospect of justifying oneâ€™s sample size now seems more daunting, not less.

Let me first note some features that I appreciated.

  • Iâ€™m glad to see resource constraints addressed as a justification for the sample size of a study. I would add that there is â€œopportunity costâ€ when one spends more time and resources on one study instead of enjoying the opportunity to spend those resources on several studies.
  • I appreciate the recommendation to perform an equivalence test instead of providing post-hoc power.
  • I think it is neat to see sample size justifications applied to qualitative research.

Again, the chief weakness of the manuscript is, in my view, that there are a breathtaking number of considerations at play. Several of the considerations have multiple sub-considerations. For example, when considering a priori power analysis, there are three different effect sizes to consider. When considering the last of these effect sizes, there are sub-subconsiderations regarding meta-analytic heterogeneity and publication bias. Although I do not doubt that all these considerations, sub-considerations, and sub-sub-considerations are important, it is hard to imagine how all these issues can be considered together to set a sample size. I would imagine that there is subjectivity, and possibly controversy, in the process by which these considerations are synthesized to perform a power analysis. As it is, it seems pretty overwhelming to try to perform a power analysis given all these considerations, and I can see researchers sticking to their heuristics so that they can set a sample size in 5 minutes instead of 5 days.

Perhaps a worked example or two would be helpful. Examples could demonstrate how the author would recommend exploring and synthesizing these many considerations, and how those considerations might be succinctly reported in a research article. The â€œ21-word solutionâ€ (Simmons, Simonsohn, & Nelson) has been successful because it gives authors a succinct template to work from â€“ would it be possible to do something similar for sample size justification? It might also be interesting to see an example of the case described in the last page in which â€œperforming the study anyway is a waste of time and/or money.â€

I would like to suggest an alternative view to the authorâ€™s regarding how and why sample sizes are justified. The considerations the author lists in this article are generally statistical, bearing on the Type I and Type II error rates of a study given some resources and SESOI. I would suggest, by comparison, that sample size is less likely to be motivated by concerns regarding error rates than it is a negotiation of sorts between the authors and the journal editor regarding how much sample size is necessary to â€œearnâ€ a publication. Ideally, most psych research will fall into Table 3, category 1: A future meta-analysis will likely be performed, so why worry about the sample sizes and error rates of individual studies? Publication decisions are gradually coming to rely less on the statistical significance of a p value, instead considering the methods of studies. In this light, justifications like â€œthis previous study used this sample sizeâ€ make sense, as these justifications are not about frequentist error rates, but about a sense of fairness in editorsâ€™ expectations regarding publishable sample sizes.

Performing a compromise power analysis on the basis of a point estimate of the population effect size feels uncomfortable to me because of the considerable sensitivity to the very precisely specified point estimate. It may be sensible when the sample size is very large or when there is a clear SESOI, but basing these calculations off an expected effect size seems awkward given the likely considerable uncertainty around that expected effect size. Maybe it is just that I cannot imagine a scenario in my discipline where a decision must be reached based on limited evidence, as social psychology has very little power to make urgent or impactful decisions.

Reviewer 3

Open response questions

Please write your review here. The author(s) will see this review. Your identity will not be revealed to the authors unless you also include your name (i.e., sign your review) in this box. It is up to you whether to reveal your identity or not, either is fine.

The manuscript under review addresses a fundamental issue in empirical research: how to determine and justify the number of collected data points. It provides an extensive overview of possible approaches to justify oneâ€™s sample size, in connection with an equally thorough review of approaches to justify oneâ€™s effect size of interest.
I very much enjoyed reading this well-written and informative manuscript. Although the issue itself and the approaches discussed are not new, this paper will make a valuable contribution to the field of psychology, no doubt. Sample size justification is a pivotal component of transparent science but it is a tough challenge in practice. The manuscript may offer guidance to researchers who are facing the challenge, thus supporting open and transparent research. This practical impact is the manuscriptâ€™s greatest strength, in my opinion, and it is one that will make it an often-read and often-cited paper. Therefore, I think that Collabra: Psychology as the official journal of SIPS is a great outlet for this paper. Nevertheless, I want to outline some concerns that I have about the manuscript in its current form, and some suggestions for improvement.

  1. I think that the manuscript is too long. For the most part, the paper has a tutorial-like character with many practical recommendations (e.g., software packages). This focus on practical applications is a strength of this manuscript and makes it particularly relevant for psychological researchers. At times, however, it reads more like a theoretical review (and the author himself refers to it as such) where the focus is less on applicability but on exhaustiveness. This is certainly informative and itâ€™s in the very nature of justification that it cannot (nor should it) be reduced to a simple set of steps like a recipe. Nevertheless, I think that the practical relevance of the paper would greatly benefit from shortening and streamlining the manuscript by removing unnecessary digressions. For example, the focus is clearly on quantitative research, so I wonder if an entire subsection on qualitative research adds to the value of the paper. Another example is p. 10: The author gives several examples for overly simplistic heuristics, where I feel that one example would suffice to make the point.

  2. The author states that all six justification approaches are valid in the sense that they give others insight into the reasons that underlie a chosen sample size (p. 2). I am not sure that this is a helpful definition of validity because itâ€™s hard to think of a way to choose a sample size which is not valid in this sense. It is important to note that the relationship between the inferential goal and the choice of a sample size differs between the justification approaches. For example, when following a heuristic, the choice might be independent of the inferential goal at hand, whereas in an a-priori power analysis, the sample size follows from the inferential goal. The value of information, however, depends only on the inferential goal and the final sample size (p. 2). Thus, when others evaluate it, it should be independent of the underlying justification. On p. 10, the author writes, â€œpeer reviewers and editors should be extremely skeptical of rules of thumb sample size justificationsâ€ â€“ why? If the sample size provides for an informative study given the inferential goal at hand, it doesnâ€™t matter how the sample size was chosen. The justification is much more important from the perspective of those who plan the study and collect the samples because it allows for a realistic evaluation of the informativeness of the study. Thus, someone who plans a study should be skeptical of heuristics because it might actually result in an uninformative study for their inferential goal. The heuristics justification might lead to an unreasonable choice of a sample size even if the inferential goal is well-specified â€“ and in that sense, I would argue, the approaches are not all valid. I think that the author implicitly differentiates between â€œvalidâ€ and â€œinvalidâ€ justifications in this sense, and it is a sense which deserves some discussion.

  3. The author introduces the value of information as a central concept to justify the sample size (p. 3). I agree that this is a highly relevant concept: A sample size justification must weigh the costs of data collection (which can also be costs on the side of the participants) against the increase in utility. I donâ€™t find the example with multiple inferential goals displayed in Figure 1 particularly helpful, however. The author argues that in such a case the value of information can be a non-monotonic function. I would go even further and claim that it is a non-monotonic function in most cases. In fact, the function displayed in Figure 1 only makes sense if we assume a non-monotonic function for each single goal. Therefore, I donâ€™t think the example with multiple inferential goals is necessary, one could demonstrate the same concept with a simpler example. Also, why is the function overall increasing? Is this based on the assumption that the decrease of the function for one inferential goal is slower than the increase of the function for another goal? Or do the inferential goals differ in utility?

  4. I was confused by the authorâ€™s usage of the term â€œpost-hoc power analysisâ€ (p. 21). The author uses the term to refer to the analysis of â€œobservedâ€ power which, as the author clearly points out, is not at all useful. However, I think this is based on an unfortunate misunderstanding of the term as it was introduced by Cohen (e.g., 1988). Like a-priori analysis, post-hoc analysis is based on a theoretical assumption about the population effect size (or smallest effect size of interest). It answers the question: Given the sample size, whatâ€™s the chance to detect a certain, theoretically meaningful effect (i.e., power). This is closely related to a sensitivity analysis. However, whereas the latter is used to identify the (range of) effect size(s) for which a test with the given sample size has a certain amount of power, the former calculates the power that the test has for a given (theoretically motivated) effect size and sample size. Therefore, to avoid confusion, I recommend the author use the term â€œretrospective power analysisâ€ (Zumbo & Hubley, 1998) instead.

  5. The notation of standardized mean differences (Cohens d/delta) is somewhat inconsistent. Typically, delta refers to a true/population effect size, whereas d refers to an observed/estimated effect size. On p. 12, for example, d is used for both true values and observed values, but in Figure 5 delta is used (where d should be used instead). I think it is important and helpful to differentiate between these two levels, so I recommend the author use delta for true values and d for observed or critical values.

  6. On p. 7, the author mentions the Bonferroni correction when researchers might reject a single hypothesis based on at least one significant result in multiple tests. Another common situation which is often overlooked is when researchers might retain a single hypothesis based on at least one non-significant outcome in multiple tests (e.g., a series of replications). In such a case, the overall Type I error might in fact be much smaller than the desired one. This situation is discussed in Francis and Thunell (2021) who suggest a â€œreverse Bonferroni correctionâ€, where the alpha for each individual test is increased to achieve the desired overall Type I error. I think this scenario and the reverse Bonferroni correction should be mentioned here.
